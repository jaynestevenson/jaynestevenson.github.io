Alameda_county_education_race <-
  1:7 %>% 
  map_dfr(function(x){
    getCensus(
      name = "acs/acs5",
      vintage = 2018,
      region = "county:001",
      regionin = "state:06",
      vars = paste0("group(C15002",LETTERS[x],")")

      select(!c(GEO_ID,state,NAME) & !ends_with(c("EA","MA","M"))) %>%
      pivot_longer(
        ends_with("E"),

More specifically, residents who are White Alone or Asian Alone are overrepresented in the highest educational attainment level and are underrepresented in the lower educational attainment levels. Conversely, residents who are Black of African American or Some Other Race Alone are underrepresented in the highest educational attainment level and are overrepresented in the lower educational attainment levels. Moreover, non-White Individuals make up only 52% of those with a bachelors degree or higher. This analysis shows that yes, there is disproportionate educational attainment by race in Alameda County.

So, access to an electronic device in addition to internet is another assumption made in this analysis. Despite these assumptions, this analysis still shows that remote learning is leaving students behind. ac_migration_flow_19 <-
   rbind(
    ac_mobility_current_18,
    ac_mobility_lastyear_19 %>% 
      filter(mobility == "Outflow"),
    ac_mobility_current_19 %>% 
      filter(mobility == "Inflow"),
    ac_mobility_current_19 %>% 
      group_by(educational_attainment) %>% 
      summarize(estimate = sum(estimate)) %>% 
      mutate(mobility = "Here this year")
  ) %>% 
  pivot_wider(
    names_from = mobility,
    values_from = estimate
  ) %>% 
  mutate(
    `External net` = Inflow - Outflow,
    `Internal net` = `Here this year` - `Here last year` - `External net`,
  ) %>% 
  select(
    `Educational Attainment` = educational_attainment,
    `Internal net`,
    `External net`,
    `Here last year`, 
    `Here this year`, 
    Outflow, 
    Inflow
  )

The results of this migration analysis show that there is a positive external net flow for the highest education levels (bachelors degree or graduate/professional degree). Interestingly, there is also a positive net external flow for the lowest education level, people in the less than high school graduate category. There is a negative external net flow for those in the high school graduate or some college or associates degree category. This division among education levels points to an interesting trend Overall, Alameda county is gaining college-educated residents and losing residents with lower education levels. The exception is represented by a positive external net flow of those in the less than high school graduate category, but the inflow of college-educated residents outweighs this significantly. Similarly, there is a large positive internal net flow in the bachelors degree and graduate or professional degree category. There is a negative internal net flow in all other categories, but the positive internal net flow dominates the trends. This analysis indicates that many residents already living in Alameda County are becoming more educated and thus leaving their old educational attainment categories.

Overall, this analysis shows that the residents of Alameda County have become increasingly educated. This poses the question, however, of what happens to those residents with lower education levels. While many people are increasing their education level internally, this analysis also shows that residents with lower education levels are also leaving the county. This indicates that there are likely negative repercussions of upward educational mobility on less educated residents that are driving them to leave, such as gentrification.
